Hip Arthroplasty

Surgical overview​

Total hip replacement (THR) or hemiarthroplasty for osteoarthritis, fractured neck of femur, or avascular necrosis. Posterior, lateral, or anterior surgical approaches.

Recommended regional technique​

Suprainguinal fascia iliaca block​

  • Targets: Femoral nerve, lateral femoral cutaneous nerve, ± obturator nerve
  • Approach: Ultrasound-guided, suprainguinal approach — needle inserted lateral to the femoral artery, directed to lift the fascia iliaca off iliacus
  • Local anaesthetic: 30–40 mL ropivacaine 0.375%
  • Onset: 20–30 minutes
  • Duration: 12–18 hours

Alternatives​

  • PENG block (pericapsular nerve group)

    • Targets articular branches of the femoral, obturator, and accessory obturator nerves
    • Ultrasound-guided injection between psoas tendon and superior pubic ramus
    • 20 mL ropivacaine 0.375%
    • Motor-sparing — may preserve quadriceps strength better than fascia iliaca block

  • Lumbar plexus block (psoas compartment block)

    • Most complete coverage of hip innervation
    • Higher risk: epidural spread, retroperitoneal haematoma
    • Typically reserved for specialist centres

  • Spinal anaesthesia

    • Commonly used as primary anaesthetic for hip arthroplasty
    • Intrathecal opioid (e.g. diamorphine 0.1–0.3 mg) provides excellent early postoperative analgesia

Key anatomy​

  • Hip joint innervation: femoral nerve (anterior capsule), obturator nerve (inferomedial capsule), superior gluteal nerve, nerve to quadratus femoris (posterior capsule)
  • Fascia iliaca compartment: between fascia iliaca and iliacus muscle
  • Suprainguinal approach achieves more reliable lateral femoral cutaneous nerve block than infrainguinal

Considerations​

  • Fascia iliaca block is the most common perioperative nerve block for hip fractures (NICE guidelines)
  • PENG block is motor-sparing but may not cover the lateral femoral cutaneous nerve (lateral thigh incision pain)
  • Posterior surgical approach: consider adding a lateral femoral cutaneous nerve component
  • For neck of femur fractures, early fascia iliaca block reduces opioid requirements and delirium risk
  • Anticoagulation must be considered — fascia iliaca is relatively compressible; lumbar plexus block is not

References​

  • Steenberg J, Møller AM. Acta Anaesthesiol Scand. 2018
  • Girón-Arango L et al. Reg Anesth Pain Med. 2018 (original PENG description)
  • NICE guideline CG124: Hip fracture management
