Total Knee Replacement

Surgical overview​

Total knee arthroplasty (TKA) for osteoarthritis, inflammatory arthritis, or post-traumatic arthritis. High postoperative pain scores — multimodal analgesia is essential.

Recommended regional technique​

Adductor canal block + iPACK block​

  • Adductor canal block

    • Targets: Saphenous nerve, nerve to vastus medialis, medial retinacular nerve
    • Approach: Ultrasound-guided, mid-thigh, deep to sartorius
    • Local anaesthetic: 15–20 mL ropivacaine 0.2–0.375%
    • Advantage: Motor-sparing — preserves quadriceps strength for early mobilisation

  • iPACK (Interspace between Popliteal Artery and posterior Capsule of the Knee)

    • Targets: Posterior genicular nerves
    • Approach: Ultrasound-guided, needle placed between popliteal artery and posterior femoral condyle
    • Local anaesthetic: 15–20 mL ropivacaine 0.2%

Alternatives​

  • Femoral nerve block — excellent analgesia but causes quadriceps weakness (fall risk)
  • Continuous adductor canal catheter — for extended analgesia over 24–48 hours
  • Periarticular local infiltration (LIA) — surgeon-delivered, complements regional techniques

Key anatomy​

  • Adductor canal: deep to sartorius, bounded by vastus medialis and adductor longus/magnus
  • Saphenous nerve travels with femoral artery within the canal
  • iPACK target zone: tissue plane posterior to distal femur, between popliteal artery and bone

Considerations​

  • Adductor canal block preferred over femoral nerve block — equivalent analgesia with preserved motor function
  • iPACK addresses posterior knee pain not covered by adductor canal block alone
  • Combine with paracetamol, NSAIDs, and gabapentinoids as part of enhanced recovery

References​

  • Terkawi AS et al. Reg Anesth Pain Med. 2017
  • Sankineani SR et al. J Arthroplasty. 2018
